Get to know 3D Models

Explore the world of 3D models! Learn what they are, where to find them, and how to get them ready for 3D printing success

> Chapter 1: What is a 3D Model?

A 3D model is a digital representation of an object in three dimensions — width, height, and depth. Unlike a flat image or drawing, a 3D model can be viewed and rotated from any angle, making it ideal for creating physical objects with a 3D printer.

Most 3D models are made up of a mesh, which is a collection of tiny triangles (called polygons) connected together to form the surface of the object. The smoother the surface, the more triangles are used.

These files usually come in formats like .STL (the most common format for 3D printing, stores only the shape of the object), .OBJ (Similar to STL, but also supports color, textures, and material data), or .3MF (modern format designed for 3D printing, storing more detailed information like color, materials, and print settings).

> Chapter 2: Finding 3D Models to Print

Before you start printing, you need something to print!


Where Do 3D Models Come From?
  • Download pre-made models from online libraries;
  • Design your own using 3D modeling software like Tinkercad, Fusion 360, or Blender (see Advances chapter)
  • Scan real-world objects using 3D scanners or smartphone apps (see Advances chapter).

Understanding how 3D models work helps you choose the right file, fix problems before printing, and even start creating your own designs.


If you’re not quite ready to design your own 3D models,don’t worry—there’s a huge community of makers who share their designs online. Many of these models are available for free and ready to download.


Popular 3D Model Repositories:

  • Thingiverse – large, community-driven platform filled with free models for all kinds of projects. Great for beginners;
  • Printables.com – offers a curated selection of high-quality models, often with detailed instructions and user reviews;
  • Cults3D – features both free and paid designs, with a focus on artistic and creative prints.;
  • Thangs.com – includes commercial-use options.

Pro Tip: Always check the license!

Some models are free to download and print, but not all allow selling or remixing. Make sure to respect the creator’s terms.

> Chapter 3: How to Use 3D Models?

Once you’ve found and downloaded a 3D model (usually in .STL or .3MF format), you’re ready to start the journey from digital file to physical object. But before you can hit “print,” there are a few important steps to follow.


1. Import into a Slicer

You’ll need a slicer—a program that prepares your model for the printer by converting it into layers and generating G-code, the instructions your printer follows.

Popular slicer programs:

  • Ultimaker Cura – beginner-friendly and supports many printers
  • PrusaSlicer – great for Prusa printers and many others
  • Bambu Studio – optimized for Bambu Lab printers

2. Adjust Print Settings

In the slicer, you can:

  • Scale, rotate, or duplicate your model
  • Set print parameters like layer height, infill, and supports

More details on these settings are covered in the “3D Printer” chapter.


3. Check for Errors

Some models may have issues like holes or bad geometry that can cause failed prints.

Helpful repair tools:

  • Microsoft 3D Builder – simple and built into Windows
  • Netfabb – online repair by Autodesk
  • Meshmixer – good for mesh editing and repair
  • Many slicers (like Cura) include auto-repair features

4. Send to Printer

Once sliced, export your G-code and transfer it to the printer using:

  • SD card or USB stick
  • Wi-Fi, if your printer supports it
  • USB cable, in some setups

Then hit "print" and watch your model come to life!


Extra Tip: Check user comments and photos where you downloaded the model. Community feedback can help you avoid issues before printing.

In the next chapter, we’ll look deeper into slicer settings and how they affect your final print.

> Chapter 4: Fundamentals of 3D Modelling (experienced)

Objective of this Chapter

This chapter will provide an overview of essential 3D modelling concepts, introduce commonly used terminology, explore free modelling tools, and guide you through fundamental geometry used in modelling for 3D printing.

Common Terms & Definitions

  • Mesh: The network of polygons (usually triangles) that form the surface of a 3D model.
  • Vertex: A point where two or more edges meet.
  • Edge: A line between two vertices.
  • Face: A flat surface enclosed by edges (typically a triangle or quad).
  • Manifold: A model that is "watertight"—no holes or non-joined edges.

Free Modelling Software: Pros & Cons

  • TinkerCAD – Beginner-friendly, browser-based. Great for simple designs and education.
    • ✅ Easy to learn and use
    • ❌ Limited in complexity and precision
  • SketchUp Free – Architectural and design-oriented modelling.
    • ✅ Good UI, useful for boxy and structural models
    • ❌ Can produce non-manifold geometry, requires cleanup
  • Blender – Advanced open-source software for modelling and sculpting.
    • ✅ Extremely powerful, supports modifiers and mesh editing
    • ❌ Steep learning curve for new users
  • Fusion 360 (Personal Use) – Parametric CAD-style modelling.
    • ✅ Precise, engineering-focused tools
    • ❌ Requires installation and account; free only for non-commercial use

Geometry in 3D Modelling

Understanding geometry is essential for creating functional and printable models. Pay special attention to:

  • Wall thickness: Thin walls may fail during printing.
  • Overhangs and bridges: These need support in slicing software.
  • Watertight models: No gaps or holes — ensure your mesh is manifold.

Getting to Know TinkerCAD & SketchUp

TinkerCAD is a drag-and-drop interface that makes it simple to build objects from basic shapes. It's ideal for quick prototyping and educational use.

SketchUp uses a drawing-based approach. You start with 2D shapes and "push/pull" them into 3D. While intuitive, ensure models are exported cleanly for printing.

Summary of Terms

  • Mesh, face, vertex, edge, manifold
  • Boolean operation (combine/subtract shapes)
  • STL, OBJ, 3MF file formats
> Chapter 5: From Sketching to Full Model (Experienced)

Introduction

Now that you’re familiar with the basics, it’s time to dive into one of the most important stages of 3D modeling: sketching. Whether you're designing a mechanical part, a custom enclosure, or a piece of art, almost every 3D model begins with a 2D sketch. This chapter will guide you through the sketching process using both external tools and the built-in features of the software included in this tutorial.

We’ll cover how to create precise, purposeful sketches using dimensions and constraints, and then explore how to transform those 2D shapes into full 3D models using common techniques. Finally, you’ll try it yourself with a simple sketching exercise inside the software.

What is Sketching & What Can You Do With It?

Sketching is the foundation of most 3D models. It involves drawing flat shapes—like rectangles, circles, or custom profiles—that define the outline of your part. These shapes are then used to build up your 3D model.

A good sketch can:

  • Define the overall shape of your object
  • Determine key features like holes, edges, and thickness
  • Serve as a base for operations like extrusion, lofting, or cutting

Sketches don’t have to be perfect at first—but learning how to control them makes the modeling process faster and more accurate.

Sketching Tools: External & In the Software

There are two main ways to start sketching:

  • External Tools – Paper and pencil or digital sketch apps (like Concepts or Procreate) are great for roughing out ideas before moving into CAD.
  • In-Software Sketch Tools – The software provided in this tutorial includes a full set of sketching tools: lines, arcs, rectangles, splines, and more. These tools allow for precise control and are designed to be the starting point for creating solid geometry.

We'll be focusing on the in-software tools going forward.

Dimensions & Constraints When Sketching

To make your sketches functional and parametric, you'll need to apply:

  • Dimensions: Set the exact size of sketch elements (lengths, angles, diameters).
  • Constraints: Lock sketch elements into place relative to each other (e.g., make two lines perpendicular, or make a circle concentric with another shape).

Using dimensions and constraints makes your sketches robust and easier to modify later.

Turning Your 2D Sketch into a 3D Model: Methods

Once your 2D sketch is complete, you can turn it into a 3D object using various modeling techniques:

  • Extrude: Extend the sketch into 3D space by giving it thickness.
  • Revolve: Spin a sketch around an axis to create circular parts.
  • Sweep: Move a sketch along a path (great for pipes or wires).
  • Loft: Blend multiple sketches to create smooth transitions.

Each method adds a different dimension to your design process.

Let’s Try It Out: Sketching Exercise

Objective: Create a base plate with a centered hole.

Steps:

  1. Open the sketching workspace and select a flat plane to draw on.
  2. Draw a rectangle (100mm x 60mm).
  3. Use dimension tools to set the exact width and height.
  4. Draw a circle in the center of the rectangle (diameter: 20mm).
  5. Apply constraints to center the circle both horizontally and vertically.
  6. Once complete, extrude the sketch to a thickness of 10mm.

You now have a simple but complete 3D model—great for practice and easy to print!

This exercise builds on everything we’ve covered: creating shapes, applying precision, and transforming a sketch into a printable object. With practice, this process becomes second nature and unlocks a whole new level of creativity in your 3D printing workflow.
